# Landscape Analysis

This document captures the real‑world environment surrounding public baby‑changing facilities in the UK. It focuses on how parents currently discover, evaluate, and navigate these spaces, and the systemic factors that shape their experience. The goal is to understand the terrain before analysing specific apps or solutions.

----

## 1. The Problem Parents are Trying to Solve

Parents and carers need to:

- **Locate** a baby-changing facility quickly, often while stressed, time-pressured, or carrying at least one child.
- **Assess** whether it's suitable:
  - Cleanliness
  - Safety
  - Accessibility
  - Space
  - Amenities
- **Trust** that the information is accurate and up to date.
- **Navigate** to the facility with minimal friction (one-handed, often on mobile data).

This is a high-stakes, low-patience scenario. The cost of failure is immediate: discomfort, hygiene issues, stress, or abandoning plans entirely.

---

## 2. How Parents Currently Find Facilities

Parents typically rely on a patchwork of sources:

- **Map services** (eg. Google Maps, Apple Maps, Bing Maps, etc) - general location info, but inconsistent tagging and unreliable reviews.
- **Council websites** - often outdated, incomplete, or buried in navigation.
- **Shopping centres** - good signage internally, but poor digital visibility.
- **Word of mouth** - local Facebook groups, WhatsApp chats, parenting forums.
- **Physical signage** - only useful once nearby.
- **Trial and error** - Walking around hoping to find something suitable.

There is **no single trusted source**. Parents assemble their own mental map over time, which doesn't help new parents, visitors, or anyone outside familiar territory.

---

## 3. The Physical Reality of Facilities

Baby-changing facilities vary dramatically in:

- **Cleanliness** - from spotless to unusable.
- **Accessibility** - many require stairs, narrow doors, or awkward layouts.
- **Space** - some barely fit a changing table; others accommodate prams.
- **Safety** - broken straps, unstable tables, or poor lighting.
- **Amenities** - bins, sinks, wipes, seating, feeding areas.
- **Availability** - some are locked or temporarily closed.

This inconsistency is the core user pain. You never know what you are walking into.

---

## 4. Data Availability and Quality

There is **no standardised dataset** for baby‑changing facilities in the UK.

Current data sources include:

- **OpenStreetMap** - patchy, volunteer‑driven, inconsistent tagging.
- **Local councils** - fragmented, non‑standard formats, rarely updated.
- **Private venues** - often don’t publish details beyond “baby changing available”.
- **Crowd‑sourced reviews** - scattered across platforms, rarely facility‑specific.

The result: **low trust** and **low completeness**.

Any product in this space must assume:

- Data starts incomplete
- Data must be user-generated or user-validated
- Moderation and accuracy are ongoing challenges

---

## 5. Behavioural Patterns and Constraints

Parents typically:

- Use their phone **one‑handed*
- Need **fast answers**, not long lists
- Prefer **visual cues** (photos, icons, quick ratings)
- Avoid typing when possible
- Operate in **low‑attention** contexts (crying baby, public space, weather, noise)
- Value **trust** over polish — they want real, recent, parent‑generated information

This shapes the UX constraints:

- Minimal friction
- No login
- Offline‑friendly
- Fast load times
- Clear, scannable information
- Simple contribution flow

---

## 6. Systemic Gaps

Across the landscape, several gaps are consistent:

- No unified, trusted directory
- No standard for reporting facility quality
- No easy way to share quick, parent‑level insights
- No lightweight, mobile‑first experience designed for one‑handed use
- No mechanism for keeping data fresh
- No visibility into accessibility or inclusivity features
- No way to surface “hidden gems” (great facilities in unexpected places)

These gaps define the opportunity space.

---

## 7. Implications for the Product

From this landscape, several design and architectural implications emerge:

- **Crowd‑sourced data is essential** — official sources are insufficient.
- **Speed and simplicity matter more than feature depth.**
- **Trust must be earned through transparency and recency.**
- **Photos and quick ratings outperform long reviews.**
- **Offline capability is a differentiator** for travel, poor signal, and emergencies.
- **Accessibility metadata** (ramps, space, door width) is a major unmet need.
- **Moderation must be lightweight but effective.**

This landscape sets the foundation for competitor analysis and the decision log.
